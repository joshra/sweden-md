# Sweden's National Parks: Conservation, Public Access, and Local Governance

> **30-second overview:** Many readers first see Swedish national parks as beautiful landscapes. In Swedish practice, though, they are also governance instruments: which lands deserve the highest level of protection, who gets to decide, who manages day-to-day use, and how public access is bounded. As of 2025, Sweden has 31 national parks, and that number reflects more than a century of institutional choices.

## Why it matters

From a Taiwan-facing perspective, the key lesson is not that Sweden has “more nature.” The deeper point is that national parks force the state to balance three pressures that rarely align on their own: biodiversity protection, public use, and local development.

Sweden's distinctiveness is not the absence of conflict. It is the decision to formalize conflict through law and governance. National parks are the strongest legal category of nature protection in Sweden, and creating one requires high-level political process. At the same time, implementation depends on local administrative capacity and long-term public trust.

## From 1909 to 2025: an institutional timeline to 31 parks

Sweden established its first national parks in 1909 and is often described as one of the earliest countries in Europe to institutionalize the national-park model. That starting point matters because it framed conservation as a state responsibility rather than only a local landscape issue.

By 2025, Sweden has 31 national parks. The newest, Nämdöskärgårdens National Park, was inaugurated in September 2025. This is a useful reminder that the map is not fixed: park policy evolves with ecological priorities, land-use pressures, and political negotiation.

## Who decides and who manages: central law, local execution

Under Sweden's Environmental Code (Miljöbalken), national parks are established by the government with the consent of the Riksdag, and the land is generally state-owned. In other words, this is not a light administrative designation; it requires constitutional and legal legitimacy.

Operational management is mainly handled by County Administrative Boards (Länsstyrelserna), which run practical stewardship: visitor information, trails, conservation measures, and local coordination. Some parks have distinct governance arrangements, including Laponia and Tyresta. The Swedish pattern is therefore layered: central legal authority with differentiated local management.

## Public access is not unlimited: boundaries inside national parks

A common misunderstanding is that the right of public access (`Allemansrätten`) means everything is allowed in national parks. It does not. Public access provides broad baseline freedoms in nature, but each national park can apply site-specific rules and stricter protections based on ecological sensitivity.

This reflects a broader Swedish norm: rights are durable only when paired with responsibility. Access remains real, but it is structured by clear limits, and those limits are part of maintaining long-term trust in shared landscapes.

## What Taiwan readers can take from this

If we focus only on imagery, Swedish parks can look romantic. If we focus on institutions, they look practical. The transferable insight is less about copying one rule and more about policy sequencing: define legal status first, clarify central-local roles second, and maintain understandable usage rules third.

Without those governance steps, conservation goals are hard to sustain under tourism pressure, development demand, and political turnover.
