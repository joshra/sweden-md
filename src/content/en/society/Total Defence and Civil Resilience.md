# Total Defence and Civil Resilience

> **30-second overview:** In Sweden, total defence means keeping society functional under crisis or war, not only strengthening armed forces. For Taiwanese readers, the key insight is practical: resilience works when roles are defined before emergencies, across government, business, and households.

## Why it matters

After moving to Taiwan, I noticed that security debates often swing between two extremes: abstract slogans or purely military metrics. Sweden's total-defence approach sits in a different place. It asks whether hospitals can keep operating, whether logistics still move, whether information remains trustworthy, and whether local institutions can coordinate under pressure.

That framing is useful for Taiwan as well. Not because the contexts are identical, but because both democracies face a shared challenge: how to prepare for prolonged risk without turning civic life into permanent emergency mode.

## From Cold War infrastructure to twenty-first-century rebuilding

Sweden developed broad civil-defence structures during the Cold War, then scaled much of them down after the threat environment shifted. Since the late 2010s, that trend has reversed. Policymakers have rebuilt planning capacity and reconnected peacetime administration with contingency planning.

This historical cycle is important. Resilience is not a one-off project. It is a long institutional process that expands or contracts with geopolitics, budgets, and political consensus.

## How the system works in practice

In Swedish policy language, total defence combines military defence and civil defence. Military institutions focus on armed threats and territorial protection. Civil-defence institutions focus on continuity: healthcare, transport, communications, power, food, water, and local government functions.

The critical point is coordination. Central government, municipalities, sector agencies, infrastructure operators, and civic organisations all carry pieces of the system. Total defence therefore tests governance quality as much as defence capacity.

## Everyday resilience is not social militarisation

For Taiwanese readers, this distinction matters. Sweden's model does not reduce preparedness to military mobilisation. It also includes household guidance, municipal exercises, agency planning, and business continuity obligations.

The practical logic is simple: clarify responsibilities in normal times so crisis response is not improvised. It is less dramatic than rhetoric-heavy security politics, but often more durable in democratic societies.

## A Taiwan-facing takeaway

If we read Swedish total defence only as a Nordic defence doctrine, we miss its most transferable lesson. Its real value is institutional: security becomes a common language across public administration, local governance, and civic education.

For Taiwan, the core question is not whether to copy Sweden's exact architecture. It is whether society can treat resilience as shared civic work, designed and rehearsed before stress arrives.
